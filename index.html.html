Standard Procedures

Core protocols for all removal types

Being on Call

A Note on These Guidelines: Every workplace is different. What follows are general principles that apply across most removal services, but your specific employer may have different expectations, shift structures, and protocols. When in doubt, defer to your employer's policies.

Shift Structure

Most working environments will have you on call anywhere from 12 to 72 hours at a time. Some operations run shorter shifts, others run longer. It depends entirely on where you work, how many employees they have, and how busy the service area is.

Some people in this industry are never off call ‚Äî particularly those who own their own operations or work in rural areas with limited staff. If you're the only removalist for fifty miles, you're always on.

Within your on-call period, you're typically designated as either primary or secondary (though some operations don't use this distinction at all).

Primary (If Applicable)
  • Handles all calls during the shift
  • Has the removal van
  • Responds to house calls, hospital calls, nursing home calls, coroner calls ‚Äî everything
Secondary (If Applicable)
  • Shows up in their own vehicle for house calls to assist
  • Typically only assists on two-person calls

Know Your Area

Don't stray too far. Being on call requires you to know your service area and stay within reasonable distance of it. If you're needed, you need to be able to get there.

Understand the geography of where you work. Know which hospitals, nursing homes, and facilities are in your area. Have a mental map of how long it takes to get to different parts of your service area. When a call comes in, you need to give an accurate ETA ‚Äî and then meet it.

The Van

If your operation uses a dedicated removal vehicle, it will be at a designated location. Coordinate with your employer or the previous shift to ensure smooth handoffs.

‚ö†Ô∏è Important: If you're taking over from someone else, confirm the van will be back on time. Don't assume ‚Äî confirm.

Your Phone

üö® Keep your phone on LOUD. Your phone is how you receive calls, call for ETAs, and navigate to the site. If you miss a call, you miss the job. During a 48 or 72 hour shift, this is especially critical ‚Äî you need to hear that phone whether you're sleeping, showering, or doing anything else.

During Your On-Call Period

When active, you await a call from dispatch (or directly from facilities, depending on your operation), and then act accordingly:

  • Nursing home or hospital: These are often solo calls ‚Äî follow your operation's protocol
  • House call: Typically requires two people ‚Äî coordinate with your partner if applicable
  • Coroner calls: Usually require a partner ‚Äî check your local requirements
Call Ownership

Generally, all calls that come in during your on-call period are your responsibility. If you get a call late in your shift and it runs over, that's part of the job.

Option: Depending on your workplace, you may be able to hand off a late call to the next person. But don't count on it ‚Äî be prepared to see every call through.

Dress Code & Appearance

First impressions matter deeply. You have to look like you know what you're doing.

  • Professional business attire is standard
  • Some operations have uniforms ‚Äî wear them properly
  • Personal hygiene is paramount
  • You're representing your employer and the profession

Mental Approach

When you're on call ‚Äî especially for extended periods ‚Äî try to relax. Don't sit around in your on-call clothes staring at the phone. It will psychologically exhaust you, especially on a 48 or 72 hour shift.

Live your normal life as much as possible, aware that at any moment you'll have to be ready to leave within fifteen minutes. Stay within your service area, stay sober, and stay ready.

‚ö†Ô∏è Non-negotiables: Don't drink. Don't do drugs. Be prepared. This applies for the entire duration of your on-call period ‚Äî whether that's 12 hours or 72.

Family Interaction, Best Practices

Understanding the Moment

Families can be very sensitive. This is often their absolute worst moment. The person they love is gone, and you're a stranger in their home during one of the most vulnerable times of their lives. Keep that in mind with every interaction.

How People Grieve

People process grief in a variety of ways. Some cry. Some are stoic. Some laugh nervously. Some get angry. Some go completely numb. All of these reactions are normal, and we must be non-judgmental of all of them.

Remember: There is no "right" way to grieve. Whatever they're feeling is valid.

The Five Stages of Grief

Understanding these stages helps you recognize what family members are experiencing and respond appropriately. Important: These are not a strict linear sequence ‚Äî people experience them in different orders, skip stages, revisit them, or feel multiple at once.

1. Denial ‚Äî "This can't be happening."

A protective mechanism that cushions the initial shock. People may refuse to believe the loss or act as if nothing has changed. It allows gradual absorption of reality, preventing psychic overload. Adaptive short-term, but can become problematic if chronic.

2. Anger ‚Äî "Why me? It's not fair!"

Anger can be directed at oneself, others, doctors, God, the person who died, or random circumstances. It's a natural response to pain ‚Äî helplessness channeled into action. This is why families sometimes snap at you. It's not about you.

3. Bargaining ‚Äî "If only... I'll do anything if..."

Attempts to regain control or postpone the inevitable. Common "what if" and "if only" thoughts. This reflects a cognitive attempt at mastery ‚Äî people negotiating to shift from fate-driven to internal control. It's somewhat regressive, echoing childhood magical thinking, but promotes reflection.

4. Depression ‚Äî "What's the point of going on?"

Deep sadness, emptiness, regret, and withdrawal. This stage involves processing the reality of the loss. This is reactive depression, not clinical ‚Äî it's the "work of mourning" where loss is internalized. It can feel overwhelming, but it's necessary grief work.

5. Acceptance ‚Äî "I can't fight it, I have to move forward."

Not necessarily happiness, but a calmer state of coming to terms with reality. It's not resignation but integration ‚Äî reframing loss into a coherent story. Allows emotional healing to begin and energy to redirect toward rebuilding life. Not everyone reaches it fully.

Shock: The Sixth Element

Shock isn't formally one of the five stages, but it's extremely common and you will encounter it constantly. It's the mind's circuit breaker ‚Äî when reality is too much to process all at once, the system simply shuts down to protect itself.

What it looks like: People in shock may feel numb, detached, or disoriented ‚Äî as if the event isn't real. Their body betrays them: rapid heartbeat, shallow breathing, dizziness, or that distant look like they're watching themselves from somewhere far away. They might stare blankly, repeat the same phrase over and over, or move through the motions like sleepwalkers. The lights are on, but nobody's home ‚Äî because home just burned down.

What's happening: Shock is the mind buying time. It's a buffer between the person and a reality too painful to absorb all at once. The body floods with stress hormones, memory gets foggy, decisions become impossible. Some people freeze entirely ‚Äî an ancient survival instinct that doesn't know the difference between a predator and a phone call at 3 AM. Later, this can become guilt: "Why didn't I do something?" But in that moment, they couldn't. The system was offline.

How it transitions: Shock fades as awareness seeps in ‚Äî usually into denial or anger. It's not a one-way door. Some people cycle back to shock during triggers: anniversaries, holidays, or when they see you arrive with a cot. Simply acknowledging shock ‚Äî "This is a lot to take in" ‚Äî can help normalize it and ease the transition.

Key Understanding: Grief is non-linear and biopsychosocial ‚Äî it's not just emotional but involves physical and mental disruptions working together. It's shaped by prior trauma, cultural background, and individual circumstances. What you see in a family is just one moment in a much longer, messier process. Meet them where they are.

Religious & Cultural Respect

Families come from a variety of religious traditions and cultural backgrounds. Some may have specific rituals, prayers, or customs surrounding death. We must respect all of them, even if they're unfamiliar to us. Don't question, don't judge ‚Äî just be present and accommodating.

When Families Are Angry

Sometimes families will be angry at us. They may snap, raise their voice, or say things that feel unfair. Let it roll off your back. Don't take it personally.

Perspective: It's better for them to yell at us than at their own family members. We can absorb that for them. It's part of the job.

Professional Composure

You are the professional. No matter what you encounter ‚Äî no matter how sad, how gory, how disturbing, or how heartbreaking ‚Äî you must maintain your composure. Keep a poker face.

This isn't about being cold or uncaring. It's about being the steady presence in the room when everyone else is falling apart. Families need someone who can hold it together. Someone who knows what to do. Someone who can guide them through this.

Remember: You cannot help a crying family if you are crying too. They need your strength, your calm, your professionalism. That's what you're there to provide.

When the scene is difficult, remind yourself why you're there: to help. Focus on the task. Focus on the family. Channel your emotions into doing your job well. There will be time to process later ‚Äî but in the moment, they need you to be their rock.

Core Expressions

With every family, make sure they hear two things from you:

  • "I'm sorry for your loss."
  • "Thank you for trusting us to take care of your loved one."

These are simple, but they matter.

The Platonic Noble Lie (By Omission)

Always being honest is a good rule. But you don't need to be too honest.

Don't tell people things they may not have seen. If someone was discovered in a bathroom, they may have only seen legs and ran away ‚Äî not the head that has an injury. Not the face that was consumed. Not the extent of what happened. They called for help and left.

Be careful with what you say. Honest, but careful. You are not lying to them. You are simply not volunteering information that would cause them unnecessary distress. There is a difference between deception and mercy.

Your job at the scene is to protect them from images they can never unsee ‚Äî even in their minds.

If you are the funeral director, have that conversation at a later time ‚Äî not at the scene, not in the raw moments. If you are in a coroner position, laws and policies may vary; consult your elected leader. No matter who you are, doing it later is better.

The Universal Truth

The same exact approach may not work with every family member. Some want to talk. Some want silence. Some want every detail explained. Some want you to just handle it. You'll have to read the room and adapt.

But this is universal: Being kind and honest works with everyone. Lead with compassion, be genuine, and treat every family with care. That will never steer you wrong.

Arrangements and the FTC

Overview

Whether you're a removalist or a funeral director, you may encounter questions about funeral arrangements, pricing, and services during a removal. Understanding the basics of funeral arrangements and the regulations that govern them helps you navigate these conversations ‚Äî and know when NOT to have them.

üö® The Core Rule: This is NOT the time for arrangements. Not for removalists. Not even for funeral directors. You don't make arrangements over mom's dead body. That conversation happens later, when the family has had time to breathe.

What is the FTC Funeral Rule?

The Federal Trade Commission (FTC) Funeral Rule is federal law that protects consumers when making funeral arrangements. It requires funeral homes to provide transparent pricing and prevents certain deceptive practices.

Key Requirements
  • General Price List (GPL): Funeral homes must provide an itemized price list to anyone who asks, in person or over the phone
  • Itemized pricing: Families have the right to choose only the services they want ‚Äî no forced packages
  • No required embalming: Embalming cannot be required except in specific circumstances
  • Outside casket acceptance: Funeral homes must accept caskets purchased elsewhere without penalty fees
  • No deceptive claims: Cannot make false claims about legal requirements for services

Why This Matters During a Removal

Families in grief sometimes ask questions or make assumptions. They're scared, overwhelmed, and worried about money on top of everything else:

  • They may ask about costs
  • They may ask what services are "required"
  • They may be confused about what happens next
  • They may have been told things by other funeral homes
  • They may be panicking about how they'll afford this

If you're a removalist: You're not a funeral director, and you shouldn't act like one. Your job is the removal. Direct arrangement questions to the funeral home.

If you're a funeral director: Even though you CAN talk about arrangements right now, you shouldn't. This is not the time. No good director tries to sell services standing over a body. Let the family grieve first. The business side comes later.

What You Can Say

The goal is to reassure them that everything will be handled, pricing will be transparent, and no one is asking for money right now:

  • "Right now, let's just focus on taking care of your loved one. We'll talk about all of that later."
  • "The funeral home will sit down with you when you're ready and go over everything ‚Äî all the options, all the pricing. It's all transparent."
  • "Nobody's asking you for any money right now. That conversation happens later, when you've had some time."
  • "You'll get a complete price list with everything itemized. You only pay for what you choose."
  • "Take your time. There's no rush on any decisions tonight."

What You Should NOT Say

  • ‚ùå Specific prices for services
  • ‚ùå What services are "required" (you may be wrong, and even if you're right, this isn't the time)
  • ‚ùå Recommendations for or against specific options
  • ‚ùå Anything that could be construed as selling or upselling
  • ‚ùå Comparisons to other funeral homes

Common Family Questions

Response: "I know that's weighing on you right now, and it's completely understandable. The funeral home will sit down with you and go over all the pricing ‚Äî everything's itemized and transparent. But let's not worry about that tonight. Right now, let's just take care of [name]."

Response: "That's a great question, and you'll have options. The funeral director will walk you through everything when you meet ‚Äî what's required, what's optional, what makes sense for what you're planning. You don't have to decide anything tonight."

Response: "I'm going to take your loved one to the funeral home now, where they'll be taken care of. Someone from the funeral home will reach out to you ‚Äî usually tomorrow ‚Äî to schedule a time to sit down and talk through everything. There's no rush. Take tonight to be with your family."

"Can we have a viewing?"

Response: "Absolutely, that's something you can do. The funeral home will go over all those options with you ‚Äî viewings, visitations, whatever feels right for your family. You'll have time to think about what you want."

"I don't know how we're going to pay for this."

Response: "I hear you, and I know this is overwhelming. The funeral home works with families on this all the time ‚Äî there are options, payment plans, different service levels. But that's a conversation for when you're ready, not tonight. Nobody's asking for anything right now."

Protecting Yourself and the Funeral Home

Making promises or claims about services, prices, or requirements can create real problems:

  • Legal liability if you quote wrong prices
  • FTC violations if you make false claims about requirements
  • Angry families if expectations don't match reality
  • Problems with your employer

The simple rule: Reassure them that everything will be handled, pricing is transparent, and no one's asking for money tonight. Beyond that, let the arrangement conference happen when it's supposed to ‚Äî later, in a calm setting, when they've had time to process.

Key Reminders:

  • FTC Funeral Rule requires transparent, itemized pricing
  • Families have the right to choose only the services they want
  • This is NOT the time for arrangements ‚Äî not for anyone
  • Reassure families: pricing is transparent, no money is due tonight
  • If you're a removalist, direct questions to the funeral home
  • If you're a director, save the business for the arrangement conference
  • Never quote prices or say what's "required" during a removal
  • Let them grieve first. The rest can wait.

Body Moving Basics

Core Principles

The Newborn Rule

Think of it like handling a newborn. You can be a little rougher than you think ‚Äî but one wrong move and there's real damage. Sometimes you need to move with more force than feels comfortable, but you also need to stay controlled. Balance being careful with being firm enough to get the job done.

The Golden Rule ‚Äî If Family is Watching

Treat them like they're alive. Smooth, gentle, respectful movements always. Any rough handling will freak the family out. Even if the body can physically handle it, the family cannot watch it.

Protect Yourself and the Body

Do what you have to do without hurting them or injuring yourself. Don't wreck your back trying to be overly gentle. Save the rougher movements for when you're behind closed doors.

‚ö†Ô∏è Never let them thump or bump to the ground. This causes postmortem injuries and will scare the family.

Universal Technique: The Tuck and Roll

This is how you get sheets, body bags, slings, or anything else under someone who is laying immobile. You'll use this constantly.

üö® BEFORE YOU START: Check the body for needles, IV lines, catheters, or anything else that might be hooked up. You don't want to accidentally get poked or unhook something unexpectedly. A quick visual check takes two seconds and can save you a lot of trouble.

  1. Position yourself on one side, your partner on the other
  2. Roll them away from you (toward your partner's side)
  3. Tuck half the sheet under them, leaving the other half hanging out on your side ‚Äî this positions the loved one in the middle of the sheet when you're done
  4. Lay them back down flat
  5. Roll them toward you (away from your partner)
  6. Your partner pulls the tucked sheet all the way through from their side
  7. Now they're centered in the sheet ‚Äî ready to move

This technique applies to: Getting sheets under bodies, positioning body bags, placing slings, setting up mobile cots ‚Äî anything you need to get underneath them.

Transfer Situations

Bed Transfers

Hospital beds have controls and need to be plugged in. Flatten the bed as much as possible, then raise it as high as it goes. Every hospital bed is different ‚Äî different switches, knobs, rail releases. Use your intuition; your gut is usually right.

  • Get your cot as close to the bed as possible
  • Match the cot height to the bed ‚Äî get them level
  • Use the sheet (tuck method or existing sheets) to slide them over
  • IMMEDIATELY secure with straps
Couch Transfers

Treat it the same as a bed. Get your cot as close to the side of the couch as possible. Use the tuck method, get parallel, slide them over.

If you can't get the cot close enough, move them in a sheet or sling and carry them to the cot. Smooth, controlled movements ‚Äî no thumping.

Recliner Transfers

It sounds odd, but recliners are a common place for people to pass away.

  • Get a sheet underneath them using the tuck method
  • Place the cot head at the foot of the recliner ‚Äî everything in one straight line
  • You and your partner each take one side
  • Move them directly straight down from recliner to cot
  • Smooth and straight ‚Äî no flopping or weird angles
  • Strap in immediately
Regular Chair Transfers

Dining chairs, office chairs, kitchen chairs ‚Äî anywhere someone is sitting upright.

Standard method:

  • Lay a sheet down next to them on the floor
  • Come from behind ‚Äî put your arms under their arms
  • Partner gets their legs
  • Go up and over onto the sheet
  • Transfer to cot, strap in

If they're too large: Place the sheet where you can, slide them off the chair onto the sheet, get the cot on the ground next to them, transfer, strap in quick.

Floor Transfers

For someone who has fallen or passed away on the floor:

  • Get a sheet, sling, or mobile underneath them (tuck method if possible)
  • Get your cot as close to them on the floor as you can
  • Go up and over from floor to cot
  • Strap in immediately
High Surface Transfers

Any elevated surface where you need to bring them down to cot level:

  • Use tuck and roll to get a sheet underneath
  • Use that sheet to transfer them down

‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL: Do NOT let them thump or bump to the ground. It causes postmortem injuries and will terrify the family. If you think you might drop them, stop and find another way. Smooth, controlled movements every time.

Fluids, Purge & Decomposition

This is the part nobody warns you about. Bodies do strange things, and you need to be prepared for anything.

Managing Fluids

Try not to make a mess ‚Äî but more importantly, try not to make a mess of yourself. Position yourself carefully. Be aware of where fluids might go when you move or reposition them. Have towels or pads ready if you anticipate purge.

Taking Care of Yourself

If you need to step out of the room for a moment to breathe, do it. There's no shame in it. Some scenes are rough. Take a second, collect yourself, and come back when you're ready. You'll be more effective if you're not fighting your own reactions.

Expect the Unexpected

Bodies can make sounds ‚Äî including post-mortem groans when you move them or strap them in. Air escaping from the lungs. It's completely normal, but it can be startling if you're not expecting it.

‚ö†Ô∏è Important: Try to strap them in when family is NOT in the room. The groans will freak them out. If it does happen in front of family, be ready to calmly explain what occurred.

The Universal Strapping Rule

üö® STRAP THEM IN IMMEDIATELY.

The second you get them to the cot ‚Äî from anywhere ‚Äî get those straps on. Don't wait, don't adjust, don't do anything else first. Any raising or lowering of the cot requires them to be fully secured. This is critical for safety.

Key Reminders:

  • The tuck and roll is your universal technique
  • Always secure with straps immediately after transfer
  • Treat them like they're alive if family is watching
  • Never let them thump to the ground
  • Use your intuition ‚Äî your gut is usually right
  • Get cots as close and level as possible before transfers
  • Smooth, controlled movements always
  • Protect yourself and the body
  • Behind closed doors, do what you need to do safely

Decomposition: A Summary

Overview

Human decomposition is the natural process where the body's tissues break down after death. This happens through two main mechanisms: autolysis (self-digestion by the body's own enzymes) and putrefaction (bacterial breakdown).

Understanding decomposition helps you anticipate what you'll encounter, how to handle remains safely, and what equipment you'll need. The timeline varies significantly based on environmental factors, but this guide covers standard conditions (room temperature, approximately 70¬∞F/21¬∞C, in air).

Important: These timelines are approximate and can overlap. Exact rates depend on many variables discussed later in this section. Every body is different.

The Types of Mortis

  • Pallor Mortis: Skin paleness as blood circulation stops (immediate)
  • Algor Mortis: Body cooling at ~1.5¬∞F per hour until ambient temperature (0-24 hours)
  • Livor Mortis: Blood pooling causes purple-red discoloration; fixed after 8-12 hours (1-12 hours)
  • Rigor Mortis: Muscle stiffening from ATP depletion (3-6 hours onset, resolves 36-48 hours)

Hours 1-2 Livor mortis (lividity) appears ‚Äî blood settles, causing purplish-red discoloration in lower body parts. Becomes fixed after 8-12 hours.

Key Terms You'll Hear

  • Pallor mortis: Skin paleness immediately after death (circulation stops)
  • Algor mortis: Body cooling (~1.5¬∞F/hour until ambient temperature)
  • Livor mortis / Lividity: Blood pooling causing purple-red discoloration
  • Rigor mortis: Muscle stiffening (starts 3-6 hours, resolves by 36-48 hours)
  • Autolysis: Self-digestion by body's own enzymes
  • Putrefaction: Bacterial breakdown of tissues
  • Marbling: Visible vein patterns from blood breakdown
  • Skin slippage: Skin separating from underlying tissue
  • Purge fluid: Liquefied tissues leaking from body
  • Adipocere: Waxy substance formed in wet/anaerobic conditions

Factors That Affect Decomposition Rate

Many variables can speed up or slow down the process. Understanding these helps you anticipate what you'll encounter.

Factors That ACCELERATE Decomposition
  • Heat: Above 70¬∞F speeds everything up ‚Äî can double rate per 10¬∞C rise
  • High humidity: Aids bacterial growth
  • Oxygen exposure: Aerobic bacteria and insects thrive
  • Insects/scavengers present: Maggots consume tissue rapidly
  • Larger body size: More tissue, retains heat longer
  • High body fat: Fat decomposes quickly
  • Naked/exposed: No barrier to insects or oxygen
  • Shallow burial: Soil microbes still active
  • Pre-existing infection/sepsis: Bacteria already present
  • Trauma/wounds: Internal tissues exposed
  • Hot/humid season: Summer accelerates significantly
Factors That DECELERATE Decomposition
  • Cold: Below 50¬∞F slows dramatically; freezing halts it
  • Dry conditions: Can lead to mummification
  • Low oxygen: Buried, underwater, sealed spaces
  • No insects: Indoors, sealed, or cold environments
  • Smaller/thin body: Less tissue to decompose
  • Thick clothing: Limits insect access and oxygen
  • Deep burial: Low oxygen and temperature
  • Embalming/antibiotics: Chemical preservation
  • Cold season: Winter slows everything

Decomposition in Different Environments

In Extreme Cold (Below Freezing)

Decomposition slows dramatically or halts entirely until thaw.

  • Hours 0-24: Rapid cooling; rigor mortis delayed or incomplete
  • Days 1-7: Minimal changes; body freezes, preserving tissues. No bloating.
  • Weeks 1-4: If thawed briefly, partial putrefaction; otherwise halted
  • Months 1+: Can remain intact for months or years (permafrost mummies). Skeletonization may take years post-thaw.
In Water (Submerged)

Slower overall due to cold, low oxygen, and reduced insects. Promotes adipocere formation.

  • Hours 0-12: Faster initial cooling
  • Hours 12-48: Rigor similar, but lividity altered by buoyancy
  • Days 2-7: Bloating delayed; skin macerates (wrinkles/softens)
  • Weeks 1-4: Adipocere (soap-like wax) forms on fatty areas
  • Months 1-6: Soft tissues last longer; skeletonization in 1-2 years vs. months in air
In Hot/Humid Conditions (Desert or Tropical)

Accelerates everything dramatically. Insects thrive.

  • Hours 0-6: Body stays warm, autolysis fast
  • Hours 6-24: Rigor resolves quicker; bloating starts early
  • Days 1-3: Active decay in hours; maggots consume tissues rapidly
  • Days 3-7: Advanced decay; mummification possible if dry heat
  • Weeks 1-2: Skeletonization complete (vs. months in standard conditions)

What This Means for You

As a removalist, understanding decomposition helps you:

  • Anticipate condition: How long has it been? What will you find?
  • Prepare equipment: Body bags, PPE, hazmat gear for decomp cases
  • Handle appropriately: Skin slippage means gentle handling; bloated bodies are fragile
  • Protect yourself: Decomp fluids are biohazards ‚Äî full PPE required
  • Manage odor: Respirators, ventilation, deodorizers
  • Set expectations: Families may not understand what time does to a body

‚ö†Ô∏è Decomposition Cases: Always use body bags. Always use full PPE. Expect skin slippage and tissue fragility. Move slowly and support the entire body. These are some of the most physically and emotionally demanding calls you'll take.

The Vicks Vapor Rub Myth

üö® DO NOT USE VICKS VAPOR RUB.

You've probably heard the rumor that rubbing Vicks under your nose helps with decomposition calls. This is bad advice. Do not do this.

Here's why:

Your brain has a natural mechanism to acclimate to smells. Given time, it adjusts and the smell becomes less overwhelming. If you're constantly giving your brain fresh smells of something else ‚Äî and opening your nasal passages more, no less ‚Äî your brain never gets the chance to adapt.

How decomp calls actually work:

  • You enter the home. It will smell.
  • The room they're in will smell worse.
  • When you move them, it will unleash a torrent of smell. You may need to remove yourself.
  • Without Vicks: You can walk back into another part of the house, and it will smell like fresh air ‚Äî even though it smelled foul when you first arrived. Your brain adjusted.
  • With Vicks: You have no escape. Every room smells like death mixed with menthol. You're trapped in a loop of dry heaving with nowhere to find relief.

Without the ability to find "fresh" air (air your brain has adjusted to), you may involuntarily vomit.

A Humbling Truth: The most hardened veterans in this industry have vomited on decomp calls. People with decades of experience. People who have seen everything. You are not better than them. Don't handicap yourself with Vicks. Let your brain do what it's designed to do.

Basic Cot Operation

Understanding the Levers
  • Pull handle = legs release
  • Let go of handle = legs lock
  • Works the same for both sides
  • Each side operates independently

The Click: When you let go of the handle, you will hear a click. That click means the legs are locked at that level. Listen for it ‚Äî it's your confirmation.

‚ö†Ô∏è BRAKES: The cot has brakes located on the wheels. Use them in most situations when transferring the deceased to ensure stability ‚Äî you don't want the cot rolling away from you while you're trying to move someone onto it. Engage brakes before transfer, release when ready to move.

Removing Cot from Van

  1. Pull the lever on one side
  2. Start pulling the cot out slowly
  3. Both legs will release as you pull
  4. DO NOT pull the cot all the way out until both legs are released
  5. Once both legs are down and locked, finish pulling the cot out
  6. You'll hear the click when legs lock into place

Raising and Lowering the Cot

The cot has multiple levels it can raise and lower to.

To Lower:
  1. Stand on the side you want to lower
  2. Pull the lever on your side
  3. Lower to the height you need
  4. Let go of the handle
  5. Legs will lock at that level (you'll hear the click)
To Raise:
  1. Pull the lever on your side
  2. Lift the cot to the height you need
  3. Let go of the handle
  4. Legs will lock at that level

Uneven Surfaces: You can adjust each side independently. One side can be higher or lower than the other if needed. Adjust as necessary depending on where you need the cot to be.

Loading Cot into Van

  1. Position the cot head first at the edge of the van
  2. Make sure you are level and even ‚Äî take your time with this
  3. Get the lip of the cot secure on the van edge
  4. Once you're confident the cot is stable and positioned correctly:
  • Pull the lever to release the legs
  • Push the cot into the van
  • The legs will naturally collapse as you push
  1. Make sure the cot is fully secured inside the van

  • Introduce yourself and your partner by name
  • Ask their names
  • Ask "How does everybody know each other?" to establish relationships
  • Identify the next of kin (the person who will sign paperwork)

  • What floor are they on?
  • Special circumstances? (Large, tight space, been deceased a while, etc.)

  • Tell the family what you're going to do ‚Äî they need to know your plan
  • If you have multiple approaches, give them Plan A and Plan B
  • Explain the physical plan (how you'll move them, what route you'll take)
  • DON'T give graphic details (e.g., if they're in a recliner, don't mention they might purge)
  • Reassure them you'll be gentle and treat their loved one with respect
  • Let them know you're about to go get equipment from the van

The goal: Keep the family comfortable while being clear about their options. Frame it carefully ‚Äî make it NOT scary.

More verbose option: "On a professional note, I'd like to add that it can be uncomfortable to watch two people you just met move someone you love far more than us.   • Cot should have pillow and quilt on it
  • If using your own sheet, bring that
  • If veteran, bring flag

Step 12: Move the Loved One

  • Bring cot inside
  • Transfer loved one to cot carefully

  • They'll tell you if they don't want that

  • Call out hazards to your partner ‚Äî thresholds, stairs, anything they can't see
  • Get deceased into van

  • Respect their space and choices

  • Now that you know the situation, go get your cot from the van
  • Position it where you need it
  • This is when you take the lead on the actual removal

  • Get the bag onto the cot
  • Cover with quilt (it'll be awkward ‚Äî bags are big and chunky)
  • Be extremely gentle ‚Äî do your absolute best to damage nothing

  • Carefully navigate to the van
  • Get them loaded
  • Leave the scene

BTP (Burial Transit Permit)

üö® IMPORTANT LEGAL DISCLAIMER

I am not a lawyer. Every state is different. Every state has some type of burial transit permit or equivalent form that must accompany a decedent during transport, but the specific requirements, forms, and procedures vary wildly by state, county, and even by who you are working for and with.

The information below is based on Indiana forms and procedures. Your state may be completely different. Consult with a lawyer or your state's regulatory board before you have serious problems. Paperwork is critically important in this industry ‚Äî get it right for YOUR jurisdiction.

What is the BTP?

The Burial Transit Permit is a legal document required for every single body you transport. No exceptions ‚Äî everybody has one. The specific name and format may vary by state, but the concept is universal: you need legal authorization to move human remains.

üö® CRITICAL: The BTP is a 3-Part Form

  • White copy ‚Äî You take this
  • Yellow copy ‚Äî You take this
  • Pink copy ‚Äî You leave this at the location

Copy Distribution

  • You bring to funeral home: White and yellow copies
  • You leave behind: Pink copy (at hospitals, nursing homes, coroner scenes)
  • If they ask to photocopy: Let them copy it, then give them the pink original
  • Never leave without the white and yellow originals

BTP Sections Breakdown

Section A ‚Äî Top Section (Decedent Information)
  • Decedent's full name
  • Time of death
  • Date of death
  • Sex
  • Age
  • Place of death (full address where they died)
  • County
  • City
  • Doctor signing the death certificate OR coroner (if coroner case)
  • Address of that doctor/coroner (called "Medical Certifier")
Section B ‚Äî Funeral Home & Signature
  • What funeral home they're going to
  • Family/Facility signature line (who signs depends on location)
Section C ‚Äî Funeral Home Representative
  • Funeral home name (again)
  • City and state of funeral home
  • Your signature as representative or mortician

Who Fills Out What ‚Äî By Location

Hospital Calls

What's already done: Top section (A) is filled out by hospital ‚Äî all decedent and medical certifier information is complete.

What you do:

  • Verify all top section information is complete
  • Fill in funeral home name, city, and state (appears in two places at bottom)
  • Make sure a nurse or hospital staff member signs the facility signature line
  • Sign your name as representative/mortician
  • Take white and yellow copies with you
  • Leave pink copy with the hospital
Nursing Home Calls

What's already done: Top section (A) is filled out by nursing home ‚Äî all decedent and medical certifier information is complete.

What you do:

  • Verify all top section information is complete
  • Fill in funeral home name, city, and state (appears in two places at bottom)
  • Make sure a nurse signs the facility signature line
  • Sign your name as representative/mortician
  • Take white and yellow copies with you
  • Leave pink copy with the nursing home
Coroner Calls

What's already done: Top section (A) is filled out by coroner's office ‚Äî coroner is listed as medical certifier.

What you do:

  • Verify all top section information is complete
  • Fill in funeral home name, city, and state (appears in two places at bottom)
  • Make sure coroner or their staff signs
  • Sign your name as representative/mortician
  • Take white and yellow copies with you
  • Leave pink copy with coroner's office
House Calls (You Do All the Work)

What's already done: Nothing ‚Äî you fill out the entire form.

Pre-Filling Your BTP Before House Calls

ALWAYS pre-fill your BTP before arriving at a house call. This makes you look prepared and professional.

What you MUST pre-fill:

  • Decedent's full name ‚Äî CRITICAL, always have this written before you arrive
  • Funeral home name, city, and state (appears in two places)
  • Date of death (from your dispatch information)
  • Address, county, city (from your dispatch information)

What you'll fill in at the scene:

  • Age
  • Time of death
  • Sex
  • Medical certifier name and address

Gathering Information at House Calls

The call you receive will often have most of the information you need to fill out the BTP.

  • Hospice nurse on scene usually has everything ‚Äî time of death, doctor info, medical certifier address
  • Police officer on scene may have time of death and other details
  • You can call the hospice nurse back if you need more information after they leave
  • Only ask the family for: Name (to verify) and age

‚ö†Ô∏è DON'T say "when did they die" ‚Äî use respectful language if asking about time. Try not to bother the grieving family with too many questions. Get information from hospice/police first.

Completing the BTP at House Calls

After you've gathered all information:

  • Complete any remaining top section information you didn't pre-fill
  • Verify funeral home name, city, and state are filled in (two places at bottom)
  • Get family member to sign the family signature line
  • Sign your name as representative/mortician
  • Take all three copies with you ‚Äî house calls are different, you keep all copies including the pink

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • ‚ùå Not pre-filling the BTP before house calls
  • ‚ùå Leaving without white and yellow copies
  • ‚ùå Forgetting to get signatures (both facility/family AND yours)
  • ‚ùå Not leaving pink copy at hospitals/nursing homes
  • ‚ùå Asking family "when did they die"
  • ‚ùå Not verifying top section is complete before you leave
  • ‚ùå Letting them keep the white or yellow if they photocopy
  • ‚ùå Forgetting to pre-fill decedent's name

Key Reminders:

  • Every body gets a BTP ‚Äî no exceptions
  • 3-part form: white, yellow, pink
  • You take white and yellow; leave pink (except house calls where you take all three)
  • ALWAYS pre-fill your BTP before house calls ‚Äî especially the decedent's name
  • House calls = you fill out everything
  • Hospitals/nursing homes/coroner = most is done, you add funeral home info and sign
  • Get signatures at every location
  • Never leave without the white and yellow originals
  • Use hospice nurse and on-scene help for information at house calls
  • Don't overwhelm grieving families with questions
  • Pre-filling makes you look professional and prepared

Emotional Lily Pads

Overview

The Emotional Lily Pads theory is a framework for understanding and navigating the complex emotional landscape of grief during removals. It helps you recognize when someone is spiraling into dark thoughts and teaches you how to gently redirect them before they sink too deep.

This is an advanced interpersonal skill that takes practice and emotional awareness. Master this technique, and you'll be able to help grieving families navigate some of their darkest moments with more grace and less pain.

The Lily Pad Metaphor

Imagine a frog sitting on lily pads in a pond.

  • The frog represents the grieving person
  • The lily pads represent individual thoughts or topics of conversation
  • The water below represents the depths of grief, despair, and overwhelming emotion

How it works: When the frog (the person) lands on a lily pad (a thought), they start out on top ‚Äî stable, present, engaged. But as they sit on that lily pad and continue thinking about that topic, they begin to sink. The lily pad can't hold them forever. The longer they stay on one thought, the deeper they sink into the water below.

Your job is to help them hop to the next lily pad before they sink completely.

How the Sinking Process Works

Stage 1: Landing on the Lily Pad (Top of the Thought)

The person starts with a thought that seems safe, neutral, or even positive:

  • "Oh, this is mom's favorite flower. She loved lilies."
  • "Dad always sat in that chair to read the newspaper."
  • "She was wearing her wedding ring when she passed."

At this stage: They're on top of the lily pad. They're having a relatively good time. They're talking to you. They're present and engaged. The thought feels manageable.

Stage 2: Beginning to Sink (The Spiral Starts)

As they continue dwelling on that same thought, connections form. Dark associations emerge:

  • "She loved lilies... they were so amazing..." ‚Üí "Well, lilies are the death flower, and the recliner she died in has lilies on it."
  • "Dad always sat in that chair..." ‚Üí "He died in that chair. He was alone. I should have been here."
  • "She was wearing her wedding ring..." ‚Üí "Dad gave her that ring 50 years ago. He's been gone for 10 years. She missed him every single day. She died of a broken heart."

At this stage: You can see them sinking. Their face changes. Their voice changes. The thought is turning dark. Racing thoughts are taking over. They're starting to spiral.

Stage 3: Fully Submerged (Total Breakdown)

‚ö†Ô∏è If you don't redirect them, they sink completely: Full emotional breakdown. Crying, screaming, collapsing. Spiraling into guilt, anger, despair. Unable to function. You're stuck there much longer. The removal becomes much harder.

This is what you're trying to prevent.

Recognizing the Sinking Process

Watch for these signs that someone is starting to sink:

Verbal Cues
  • Their sentences get longer and more intense
  • Their voice changes pitch or volume
  • They start saying "should have" or "if only"
  • They make dark connections or associations
  • They repeat the same phrase over and over
  • Their language becomes more emotional
Physical Cues
  • Their breathing changes (faster, shallower, or catching)
  • Their face tightens or contorts
  • They grip something (table, chair, your arm)
  • They look away or stare blankly
  • Their body tenses up
  • Tears start forming
Emotional Cues
  • The mood in the room shifts
  • You can feel the energy changing
  • They're going somewhere dark
  • You sense they're about to break down

Trust your instinct. You'll feel it when they start to sink.

How to Get Them to Another Lily Pad

When you see someone starting to sink, your job is to gently redirect them to a new thought ‚Äî a new lily pad ‚Äî before they go under completely.

Redirection Techniques

1. Casual Topic Change

  • Introduce a new, neutral topic smoothly
  • "Speaking of flowers, do you have a garden at home?"
  • "That's a beautiful photo on the wall ‚Äî where was that taken?"
  • Make it feel natural, not abrupt

2. Shift to Paperwork

  • "I just need to verify a couple of things on this form..."
  • "Can you help me with the spelling of their middle name?"
  • Gives them something concrete to focus on
  • Pulls them out of the emotional spiral

3. Ask a Practical Question

  • "Would you like a glass of water?"
  • "Is it okay if I open this window for some air?"
  • "Would you mind helping me move this table out of the way?"
  • Redirects to immediate, manageable tasks

4. Bring Up a Different Memory

  • If they're sinking on a dark memory, redirect to a lighter one
  • "What was their favorite thing to do on weekends?"
  • "Did they have any funny habits that made you laugh?"
  • Gentle, not forced

5. Physical Movement

  • "Why don't we step outside for some fresh air?"
  • "Can I get you something from the kitchen?"
  • Movement can break the spiral
  • Changes the environment and the thought pattern

6. Validate, Then Redirect

  • Acknowledge what they said, but don't let them dwell
  • "That must be really hard. I can see how much you loved them."
  • Then redirect: "I want to make sure I take good care of them ‚Äî can you tell me their full name one more time?"
  • You're not dismissing their pain, you're helping them manage it

The Art of Gentle Redirection

Key Principles
  • Be subtle: Don't make it obvious you're redirecting them. It should feel like a natural conversation flow. If it feels forced, they'll resist or feel dismissed.
  • Be compassionate: You're not cutting them off because you don't care. You're helping them not drown in their grief. Your intention is to protect them from total breakdown.
  • Be patient: Sometimes it takes a few tries. They might sink again on the next lily pad. Keep gently guiding them.
  • Be present: Stay engaged and attentive. They need to feel heard, not rushed. Balance moving forward with giving them space.

Why This Matters

For them:

  • Prevents total emotional breakdown during an already traumatic time
  • Helps them maintain some control and dignity
  • Allows them to get through the necessary tasks
  • Protects them from drowning in the worst moments of their grief

For you:

  • Keeps the removal moving forward
  • Prevents situations where you're stuck for hours
  • Protects your emotional well-being
  • Allows you to do your job effectively
  • Remember: Move slow to move fast (see Extreme Emotional Situations section)

For the situation:

  • Maintains a functional environment
  • Allows other family members to cope
  • Prevents one person's breakdown from triggering everyone else
  • Creates space for grief without total chaos

Common Lily Pads ‚Äî Things That Cause Sinking

Different people sink on different lily pads. Here are common ones:

  • Objects in the room: "That's the blanket they were wrapped in..." ‚Üí Sinking: "...the last thing they touched. They were so cold. I should have been here to keep them warm."
  • Photos: "That's from our vacation last year..." ‚Üí Sinking: "...we were so happy. I didn't know it would be the last time. I didn't say enough. I wasted so much time."
  • Timing: "It happened so fast..." ‚Üí Sinking: "...I wasn't ready. I never got to say goodbye. There's so much I never said. I failed them."
  • Medical details: "The doctor said it was peaceful..." ‚Üí Sinking: "...but how do we know? What if they were in pain? What if they were scared? What if they called for me and I wasn't there?"
  • Guilt spirals: "I should have..." ‚Üí Sinking: "...visited more, called more, been there more. I was selfish. I took them for granted. This is my fault."
  • Religion/Afterlife: "At least they're with God now..." ‚Üí Sinking: "...but what if they're not? What if there's nothing? What if I never see them again? What does any of this mean?"

When you hear these starting, gently guide them to another lily pad.

Result: You successfully navigated her through multiple lily pads without letting her sink completely. She was able to maintain composure, complete the necessary tasks, and say goodbye with dignity.

The Human Puppet Technique

üö® WARNING: This is an advanced technique that sounds horrifying and looks horrifying. DO NOT do this in front of family members. It will scare them. This technique should only be used in extreme situations and away from anyone who is not trained in removals.

What is the Human Puppet Technique?

The human puppet technique is an advanced version of the tuck and roll technique designed for extreme decomposition situations. It allows you to maneuver loved ones safely without causing further damage to the body or exposing yourself to hazardous conditions.

Think of it as: An advanced tuck and roll technique using sheets as handles to control movement.

When to Use This Technique

This technique is ONLY for extreme situations:

  • People in water: Waterlogged bodies, bodies recovered from drowning scenes
  • Extreme decomposition: Advanced stages, bodies that have been deceased for extended periods
  • Cases of serious skin slip: When skin is separating from the body, high risk of tissue damage during movement

What is Skin Slip?

Skin slip occurs when a person has been deceased for so long that their skin begins to separate from the underlying tissue.

What it looks like: The skin separates from the body similar to how fried chicken skin separates from the meat. The outer layer can slide off or release. Everything inside can release with it. Can be extremely messy and unsettling.

When it happens: Extreme decomposition cases, bodies deceased for very long periods, water-related deaths, bodies in warm/moist environments.

‚ö†Ô∏è Why it's dangerous: Traditional handling methods can cause the skin to tear or slide off. Can damage the body and make identification harder. Creates biohazard exposure for you. Extremely distressing if family were to witness.

The Technique ‚Äî Step by Step

Equipment Needed
  • 4 sheets (sometimes 5)
  • Your partner
  • Body bag (usually the destination in these cases)
The Process
  1. Tie sheets to extremities: Take one sheet and tie it securely to each wrist. Take one sheet and tie it securely to each leg. Optional: Sometimes use a fifth sheet in the middle for additional support and control.
  2. Position yourselves: You and your partner each take sides. Each person controls two sheets (one arm, one leg on their side). Coordinate your movements.
  3. Maneuver using the sheets: Use the sheets as handles to control movement. The sheets allow you to move the person without direct contact with fragile tissue. You can properly maneuver without breaking anything, causing skin slip, creating additional damage, or exposing yourself to direct contact with decomposition.
  4. Move to destination: Most of the time you're moving them into a body bag. Use the sheets to carefully lower or position the body. Maintain control throughout the entire movement.

Why This Technique Works

Advantages:

  • No direct contact with fragile, decomposing tissue
  • Distributed support across multiple points (wrists, legs, possibly torso)
  • Better control than trying to lift or carry directly
  • Prevents further damage to the body
  • Protects you from direct exposure
  • Allows maneuvering in tight corners or difficult spaces

When traditional methods fail: Traditional tuck and roll can cause skin to tear off. Direct lifting can cause limbs to separate. Standard handling can create additional mess and damage. This technique minimizes all of those risks.

Critical Safety Reminders

üö® Never use in front of family. This looks disturbing. It will terrify and traumatize family members. Use only when family is not present. If family insists on staying, you must use different methods.

  • Use full PPE: Gloves are mandatory. Consider additional protective equipment for extreme decomp. Body bags are essential. See PPE & Sanitation section.
  • Communicate with your partner: Coordinate every movement. Count off: "On three, we lift." Move slowly and deliberately. One person should take the lead on calling movements.
  • Assess before you start: Is this technique actually necessary? Could a simpler method work? What's the destination? What's the path ‚Äî are there obstacles?
  • Clean up thoroughly: Extreme decomp situations require extensive sanitation. Dis-Spray everything. Consider disposing of sheets used. Shower and change clothes immediately.

When NOT to Use This Technique

Don't use if:

  • Family is present or nearby
  • The body is not in extreme condition
  • Standard tuck and roll will work
  • You're not trained and comfortable with the technique
  • You don't have a partner

Use standard methods instead for: Normal removals, bodies in good condition, when families are watching, hospital and nursing home pickups.

Key Reminders:

  • This technique sounds and looks horrifying ‚Äî keep it hidden from family
  • Only for extreme situations: water, advanced decomp, skin slip
  • Skin slip = skin separating from body like fried chicken skin
  • 4-5 sheets tied to extremities for control
  • Prevents further damage to fragile bodies
  • Protects you from direct exposure
  • Allows maneuvering in tight spaces
  • Always use with a partner
  • Communicate every movement
  • Clean everything thoroughly after
  • When in doubt, consult dispatch

What is the Patch Adams Technique?

The Patch Adams technique is named after the movie "Patch Adams" and is used to break people out of operating on autopilot.

When People Operate on Autopilot
  • Sometimes people autopilot in a really dangerous way
  • Sometimes it's a really big fight
  • Sometimes there's a scenario going on and everyone is deeply into it
  • You can see everyone is on autopilot, consumed by the situation
  • You need to break them out of it

The Theory Behind It

How It Works

If you do something completely out of character or strange, it will shock the person or people around you. This shock:

  • Makes them pay attention to you
  • Makes them stop what they're doing
  • Returns them back to reality for a second
  • Gives you back control of the room
Why It Works

Because we are so professional, calm, somber, and don't joke around (although we are friendly), people have certain expectations of us. When we break those expectations dramatically, it's jarring enough to snap them out of whatever emotional state they're in.

When to Use

  • Extreme situations only
  • Family is fighting (physically) and yelling
  • Violence in general not directed at you
  • Situation is escalating dangerously
  • You've lost control of the room
  • Nothing else is working

You calmly sit down on the couch and start smoking a cigarette.

What Happens

  • Their autopilot breaks

  • "Okay, then you can go on talking."

The Result: Things that jarring can literally snap the family out of whatever mood they're in. The shock breaks the cycle. You now have their attention and can redirect the situation.

Total Denial / Resurrection Attempts

Understanding Denial as a Spectrum

Denial is one of the stages of grief. When someone loses a loved one, denial is a natural part of the process. But sometimes, people get trapped inside that stage in an extreme version that can complicate removals.

Like most things in this guide, denial is not black and white. It exists on a spectrum ‚Äî from minor, healthy coping mechanisms to total, reality-rejecting states that prevent you from doing your job.

Minor Denial ‚Äî Normal Grief Response

Small behaviors that show the person is processing death in stages:

  • Doing chores for the person who has passed
  • Giving them something to bring with them
  • Making sure they are warm, comfortable
  • Folding their clothes, tidying their space

These are very minor forms of mental denial. Normal coping mechanisms. Don't comment on it ‚Äî or if you do, just say that it's nice. Let them process naturally. This doesn't affect your job.

üÉè Pro Tip: Often in winter, I will use this as a form of comfort as I leave. When I say I'm going to cover them for privacy, I'll add that it's also a bit cold outside, and we can stay warmer that way. Seems to help.

Friendly Denial ‚Äî Not Blocking Removal

The person is in a state of denial, but they're not directly impeding the removal.

Example: Sitting in the same living room as their deceased friend, loved one, spouse. The deceased is in a recliner, clearly dead. They're acting like the person is not dead at all ‚Äî still talking to them, tapping their shoulder. But they sign the paperwork knowing you're about to take them. Everything proceeds normally, but emotionally they're in denial.

Part of them knows what's happening. Part of them is refusing to accept it. Both can be true at the same time.

How to Handle Friendly Denial: Do NOT directly confront this before you move them. They're cooperating. They're allowing the removal. Forcing them to accept reality right now won't help ‚Äî it will only cause a breakdown that delays everything. Let it be. Let friends and family talk to them when you leave. They can come into acceptance on their own timeline. The funeral director will talk to them later. This isn't your job to fix.

Active Denial ‚Äî Impeding the Removal

In other types of denial ‚Äî especially with children ‚Äî the denial becomes active and directly interferes with the removal.

Examples:

  • A parent still trying to feed a dead child ‚Äî won't accept they're gone
  • It's Christmas Day. Dead person on the couch. Family member has put tape on the dead person's fingers, using them to tape Christmas presents. Acting like they're participating in Christmas.
  • Continued CPR long after it's futile
  • Shaking the body, screaming at them to wake up
  • Physically blocking you from approaching

When they are actively impeding the removal ‚Äî physically involving the deceased in activities, refusing to let you approach, insisting the person is fine ‚Äî you can't do your job. You unfortunately have to confront the fact that they're dead.

The Consequence ‚Äî Prepare Yourself

üö® When You Break Through Denial: They will snap very hard into one of the other stages of grief, and it will rarely be acceptance first. They'll jump to anger, or depression, or bargaining. They'll crash hard. They'll likely cycle through multiple stages rapidly ‚Äî screaming, crying, anger directed at you, collapse, breakdown, refusal. All of it at once. You have to accept this: breaking through denial is sometimes necessary, and when you do it, you're going to cause an emotional explosion.

The Technique: Living Within the Illusion

Step 1: Meet Them Where They Are

To confront denial, you will first have to live within the illusion. If they're on the ground with the deceased, sit next to them. Hang out. Talk to them like nothing is going on. Enter their reality temporarily. Don't immediately challenge it. They're kind of already broken from reality ‚Äî shocking them will make it worse. You need to build trust first.

Step 2: Use the Patch Adams Technique

A Patch Adams technique might be necessary here ‚Äî doing something unexpected to break the pattern gently. See the Patch Adams section for details.

Step 3: Gentle Nudges Toward Reality

You sit there with them for a moment in the illusion. Then: place a hand on the decedent. Moment of silence. Say something small and observational:

  • "I think they're a little cold."
  • "Their hand feels different."
  • "They're not breathing very well."

Tiny, extremely small nudges in this direction ‚Äî most of the time ‚Äî will not be too unsettling and can get you where you need to go. You're introducing reality gently. Letting them come to the realization themselves. Guiding them without forcing.

Step 4: Avoid Heavy Stark Realizations

What NOT to say:

  • "They're dead."
  • "You need to accept this."
  • "They've been gone for hours."
  • "This is ridiculous, they're obviously dead."

Too harsh. Too shocking. Will cause immediate breakdown. Won't help. Small observations work better than harsh truths.

When Denial Turns Into Barricade

There is a point where total denial can escalate into an open barricade situation ‚Äî they refuse to let you near the body, physically block you, lock themselves in a room, become aggressive about protecting the "illusion."

See the Barricade Situations section for full handling protocols.

Attempted Resurrection Practices

In extreme cases, family members may attempt to resurrect the person through spiritual or supernatural means ‚Äî witchcraft practices, voodoo, spiritual rituals, religious ceremonies, cultural traditions. Although generally more associated with certain cultural and religious practices, it does sometimes happen across various backgrounds.

Why these are hard to handle: These situations involve deeply held cultural and religious beliefs that can be hard to speak about rationally. These are tied to their worldview, their spirituality, their culture. Logic won't work here. They genuinely believe the person can come back, the ritual will work, death is not final in this way. You're not dealing with denial in the psychological sense ‚Äî you're dealing with a spiritual belief system.

How to Handle Resurrection Attempts: You cannot do this alone. It's highly possible you'll need a team effort ‚Äî family members who share the culture but understand the situation, religious or spiritual leaders from their community, everyone around you who can help bridge the gap. Be respectful of their beliefs. Don't mock or dismiss the practices. Don't argue about theology. Focus on the practical need for removal. Let family members or spiritual leaders explain why the body must be moved, that the ritual can continue elsewhere, that removal doesn't prevent resurrection. You cannot navigate this effectively yourself. Be patient. This may take time.

The Transition

Eventually, denial breaks. You'll see it happen ‚Äî a shift in their eyes, a collapse, a wail that sounds different from before. This is when grief floods in. When this happens:

  • Be there. Don't back away.
  • Let them cry, scream, collapse.
  • Offer physical support if appropriate (a hand on the shoulder, helping them sit down).
  • Give them time with the body now that they've accepted reality.
  • Then, gently, begin the removal process.

Remember: You are witnessing one of the most painful moments a human being can experience ‚Äî the moment reality forces its way through denial. Treat it as sacred. Your patience in this moment can shape how they process this loss for years to come.

Barricade Situations

Overview

A barricade situation occurs when someone takes their deceased loved one into another room, locks themselves inside, and refuses to let anyone take the deceased away. They may be spending more time with them, protecting them, or simply unable to accept what's happening.

There are many reasons people barricade, but the core issue is always the same: they are trying to stop time.

Who Barricades?

While anyone can barricade, certain patterns emerge:

  • Parents with young children
  • Often opposite-sex parent and child (son barricades when mom dies, daughter when dad dies)
  • Young lovers

These relationships often have the deepest attachment bonds and the most difficulty accepting the finality of death.

The Psychology: Trying to Stop Time

When tragedy strikes, it feels like the whole world should stop. But it doesn't. And that is terrifying.

The reality they're facing:

  • People are still ordering pizzas
  • Commercials are still playing on TV
  • The dog still needs taken out
  • The laundry still needs folded
  • Everyone else is just... moving on

How can the world just keep going when the most important person in their life is gone? How can time keep moving forward when their entire world has ended?

The barricade is their answer: If they lock the door and refuse to let anyone in, if they hold onto their loved one and won't let go, maybe ‚Äî just maybe ‚Äî they can force time to stop. They can make the world pause. They can prevent this moment from ending and the next terrible moment from beginning. They are literally trying to stop time itself.

The Conflict in the Room

Two opposing forces are at work:

Everyone else is trying to push time forward:

  • Police want to clear the scene
  • Coroner needs to take the body
  • EMTs need to finish their work
  • Hospice nurse needs to complete paperwork
  • You need to do the removal
  • Everyone has a timeline, a schedule, a next step

The barricaded person is forcing time to STOP:

  • They will not move forward
  • They will not let go
  • They will not open the door
  • They are holding the line against time itself

This creates an impossible standoff ‚Äî unless you change your approach.

Step 1: Accept That Time is Stopping

Don't try to push time forward. Everyone else is already doing that. The barricaded person is being pushed from all sides to "move on," "let go," "accept reality."

You need to do the opposite.

What this looks like:

  • Stop rushing
  • Stop trying to make them hurry
  • Stop treating this like a problem to solve quickly
  • Accept that time is stopping
  • Stop time with them

Why this works: You become the only person not pushing against them. You become an ally instead of another force trying to take their loved one away. You create space for them to breathe. You validate that stopping time ‚Äî even just for a moment ‚Äî is okay.

Step 2: Use the Patch Adams Technique

The barricaded person is locked in a loop of grief and fear. To break through, you may need to do something unexpected that gets their attention.

Example ‚Äî The Teenager with Posters:

You arrive at a barricade situation. A teenage son has locked himself in his room with his deceased mother. You look around ‚Äî the walls are covered in Macho Man Randy Savage posters.

The Patch Adams Move: Talk like Macho Man Randy Savage through the door. "Hey, brother. Let me in to talk to you." Use his voice, his catchphrases. Make it unexpected, jarring, even slightly absurd.

Why this works:

  • It breaks the pattern of grief
  • It's so unexpected that it pulls them out of their emotional loop
  • It shows you SEE them ‚Äî their interests, their personality, who they are
  • It creates a connection point
  • It gives them permission to engage because you're not just another authority figure demanding they open the door

Step 3: Let Them Know You're Okay With Time Stopping

Through the door, in a calm, genuine voice:

  • "I know this feels impossible right now."
  • "I know the world is moving too fast."
  • "I'm not here to rush you."
  • "We can take a minute. We can stop for a second."
  • "I'm okay with time stopping for a little while."
  • "You don't have to do this alone."

Ask about the decedent:

  • "Tell me about them. What were they like?"
  • "What's your favorite memory of them?"
  • "How did you two meet?"
  • "What did they love to do?"

This gives them permission to feel what they're feeling. It removes the pressure. It makes you safe instead of threatening. It creates an opening for communication. And asking about the decedent shows you care about the person they lost ‚Äî not just the task at hand.

Step 4: If They Won't Open the Door (Hours Pass)

Sometimes, despite your best efforts, they will not open the door. Hours pass. The situation becomes untenable.

Police intervention may become necessary. If it's been hours and there's no progress, police may need to physically break down the door. This is a last resort.

How to handle this:

  • Ask police to break the door in
  • But then let YOU come in first ‚Äî not the police
  • Give yourself a chance to talk to them before anyone else enters

Why this matters: Police breaking in is already traumatic. If police immediately grab the body, it's worse. If YOU come in first, calmly, you can still de-escalate. You can still be the compassionate presence. You can still explain options.

Step 5: Explain the Reality (Gently)

Once you're in or they're talking, you need to explain why the body cannot stay where it is. Do this gently, compassionately, and honestly.

The facts:

  • The body cannot remain where it is
  • It will decompose
  • Things will happen ‚Äî odor, fluids, health hazards
  • Many problems will arise
  • Most people don't own land where they could bury someone
  • You can't bury someone in an apartment
  • In most states, the body legally has to be removed

The body has to go. That's just the truth. But you can deliver that truth with compassion.

Step 6: Present the Choice (Without Threats)

There are two options:

Option 1: Come with you

  • You can be gentle, gracious, compassionate
  • You can stop time for a moment with them
  • You can let them have a few more minutes
  • You can do this with dignity
  • You can treat their loved one with respect
  • You can move slowly

Option 2: The police

  • If they refuse, police will eventually have to intervene
  • It won't be gentle
  • It won't be gracious
  • It will be forced

üö® CRITICAL ‚Äî What NOT to Say:

  • NEVER say "The police are going to rip the body out of your hands"
  • NEVER say "They'll force you to let go"
  • NEVER say "You don't have a choice"
  • NEVER use any language that sounds like a threat

This will scare them, position you as the bad guy, break any trust you've built, and make the situation worse.

What to say instead:

  • "I can help you do this in a way that honors them."
  • "I know this has to happen. Let me help you do it on your terms."
  • "I'm not here to take them away from you. I'm here to help you take care of them."
  • "You can have a few more minutes. Then we'll do this together."

Step 7: Remember ‚Äî You Are Not the Bad Guy

You are not the bad guy.

  • You are not ripping the body out of their hands
  • You know you wouldn't do that
  • You're offering them the compassionate alternative
  • You're the one stopping time with them
  • You're the one who sees their pain

But this is the reality:

  • The body does have to be removed
  • That's just the legal and practical reality
  • It's not your rule ‚Äî it's just what is
  • You're navigating an impossible situation

If police have to intervene and it gets ugly, that's not on you. You tried. You offered the better option. You did your job with compassion.

But never be the person who threatened it. Don't position yourself as the enforcer. Don't make it an ultimatum. Don't be the one who makes them feel cornered. Be the person offering the way through, not the person forcing them through.

Why This Approach Works

Stopping time with them:

  • Makes you an ally, not an adversary
  • Gives them permission to grieve
  • Creates space for them to process
  • Validates their feelings

The Patch Adams technique:

  • Breaks their emotional loop
  • Creates unexpected connection
  • Shows you see them as a person
  • Gives them a way to engage

Presenting reality without threats:

  • Maintains trust
  • Keeps you as the compassionate presence
  • Gives them agency (choice between you and police)
  • Allows them to maintain dignity

Being honest:

  • They know the body has to go
  • Pretending otherwise would be dishonest
  • But you can be honest AND compassionate
  • You can speak the truth gently

Key Reminders:

  • Barricades happen when people try to literally stop time
  • Most common with parents and children (often opposite-sex)
  • Everyone else pushes time forward ‚Äî you need to stop it with them
  • Use Patch Adams technique to create unexpected connection
  • Let them know you're okay with pausing
  • Body legally has to be removed in most cases
  • Offer the choice: gentle removal with you vs. forced removal by police
  • NEVER threaten what police will do ‚Äî just present reality compassionately
  • You are not the bad guy ‚Äî you're offering the better path
  • If police intervene, ask to enter first before they do
  • Stopping time for a moment gives them space to accept what must happen next

Special Decedent Cases

Overview

The reality of this job is that you really don't know what you're walking into ‚Äî ever. You can't account for anything at all. Every situation is totally different, and there's no way to account for everything. We can't possibly list every single weird situation you'll encounter ‚Äî it's just not possible.

Know this going in: Expect the unexpected.

This section covers some of the most common special cases you'll encounter and how to handle them professionally and safely.

Case #1: Bariatric Individuals (Oversized People)

This is one of the biggest and most common challenges you'll face.

Common Situations

Bariatric individuals often die young. Family calls 911, EMS attempts resuscitation, pulls them off the bed to perform CPR, places intubation tubes. By the time you arrive, they're usually on the floor.

Equipment & Techniques
  • The mobile cot is extremely useful for weight distribution and getting them off the floor
  • The partial human puppet technique is acceptable to use in front of family (when it's not a decomp situation) for better leverage and maneuvering in awkward locations
Speaking with Family

üö® What NOT to do:

  • Don't react visibly to their size, make faces, or show shock
  • Don't say "I don't know if I could do that"
  • Don't look scared or uncertain
  • Don't make the family uncomfortable about lifting their loved one

What TO do:

  • Remain professional, be confident, treat it like any other call
  • Poker face is essential
  • Be reassuring that you can handle it
  • Never look scared ‚Äî this is very important

The family is already dealing with their loved one's death. They don't need to feel embarrassed about their size. Your confidence helps them feel their loved one is being treated with dignity.

Extreme Weight Situations (600+ Pounds)

When to call fire department: Try first, but if it's a serious "My 600 Pound Life" situation and you genuinely cannot safely move them, call the fire department. Don't hurt yourself.

If you call fire department:

  • Fire departments show up like a bull in a china shop ‚Äî loud, rough, and insensitive
  • You must take control of the room
  • Tell them specifically what you need help with ("We need help guiding them onto the cot")
  • Direct their actions ‚Äî don't let them take over
  • Make sure they don't leave before the person is loaded into the van (they'll try to leave as soon as the person is on the cot)
  • Ask them to follow you back to the funeral home (you'll need them there too)
If Family Wants to Help

Sometimes family has a crew of big, strong, young men who are emotionally capable and determined to help. There's no shame in letting family members help with the removal ‚Äî it can be helpful and meaningful for them. They want to feel useful during a helpless time.

BUT ‚Äî you still take the lead. Direct them on what to do, maintain safety, and stay in charge.

Case #2: Decomposition & Extreme Gore

These situations test your professionalism, composure, and technical skills.

What You'll Encounter

Bodies totally covered in maggots, blood everywhere, mummified remains, skin slip, extreme odors, body fluids throughout the scene.

Fun fact (terrifying edition): If you're in a room with an intense amount of maggots, you can actually hear them. They sound like Rice Krispies.

Preparation & Equipment
  • Stock up on multiple sheets and body bags before you arrive
  • Every funeral home will want decomposed bodies in a body bag ‚Äî especially if any bugs are present (roaches, maggots, bed bugs, anything)
  • Maggot infestation in a funeral home storage location is very serious
  • The human puppet technique is super helpful for decomp situations
  • Most of the time, you're going to want to get these people into a bag as quickly as possible
If Family is Present

Managing the scene:

  • Use the sheet they were covered with (or an extra sheet/quilt) and leave it over the spot where bodily fluids are left behind
  • Warn them professionally about what they might see when they pull back the sheet or walk back into the room
  • Use correct, professional wording ‚Äî this helps them keep their composure and prepare mentally

If family wants to see the deceased:

In a way, it is their right to see their loved one. But your job is to control everyone's emotions and be honest. Explain that natural processes have happened and the body may not look the way they remember.

‚ö†Ô∏è Don't use the word "scary" ‚Äî don't put that image in their head if it wasn't already there.

Suggest alternatives:

  • "They may not want to see them right now"
  • "They should wait and talk to the funeral director"
  • "There may be restorative practices that can help"
  • "It might be better to remember them as they were"

Offer ways to still spend time:

  • Cover them entirely with the quilt
  • Family can spend time this way
  • Offer to say a prayer together
  • They can talk to their loved one through the quilt
  • This provides closure without traumatic imagery

Important: Try to avoid them seeing extreme decomp. It can cause significant, lasting emotional distress that interferes with their grieving process.

Your Professional Composure

üö® CRITICAL: You are the professional. You are the one who knows what they're doing. You are the stability in the room. The family may be leaning on you the entire time you're there.

Control your reactions:

  • No retching or dry heaving
  • No facial expressions (grimacing, disgust, shock)
  • No crying in front of the family
  • No visible reaction to the smell, gore, or decomposition

If you lose your composure, the family will lose theirs. You set the tone for the room. Your professionalism helps them feel their loved one is in good hands. Use your poker face. Save your reactions for when you're alone in the van.

Case #3: Waterlogged Bodies

Waterlogged bodies present unique challenges due to skin slip, weight, and fluid complications.

What Counts as Waterlogged

Bodies in bathtubs, pools, ponds, lakes, showers (still running water), or even just a hand in a bowl of cereal ‚Äî any type of water contact counts.

What Happens
  • Waterlogged bodies can inflate and deflate
  • Can turn into "a bowl of chunky soup" inside the bathtub
  • Skin slip becomes a serious problem
  • Everything inside is more liquid than before
  • Weight increases significantly due to water absorption
Techniques for Removal
  • The human puppet technique can be vital for getting people out of a bathtub
  • Sometimes you can tuck and roll them while they're still in the tub using a body bag
  • Now your body bag is entirely full of water ‚Äî cut the bottom with your knife to let it drain
  • This is why you should bring a knife
Handling Wet, Slippery Conditions

The bathroom is very wet and slippery with water everywhere in a tight space.

  • Use everything to your advantage: the toilet, sink, towel rack, edge of the tub ‚Äî anything for stability
  • Make sure you have shoes with good tread
  • Stand on a bathroom mat if available
  • Throw sheets on the floor for extra traction ‚Äî they absorb water and give you better footing
  • You may have to put a foot in the bathtub or brace yourself around it
  • Use a sheet for grip and control, or a body bag under them if not too much water

‚ö†Ô∏è Don't let them slip out of your hands and hit their head ‚Äî could create more gore and postmortem damage.

Case #4: Hanging Cases

Bodies found hanging require careful handling to prevent injury and preserve evidence.

Getting Them Down

üö® Never just cut them down ‚Äî they will flop directly to the ground and cause postmortem damage.

  • Fire department or police are often already there and can help using ratchets
  • You need somebody holding and supporting the body before you cut the rope
  • Support first, then cut carefully, bring them down with control
Handling the Rope

If going to funeral home:

  • Remove the rope from around their neck before transport

If going to coroner's office:

  • Treat it like a coroner call
  • Do NOT touch anything
  • Leave the rope on
  • Don't interfere with evidence

Case #5: Outdoor Bodies

Bodies found outside present environmental and logistical challenges.

Where They're Found

Golf courses, backyards, back decks, inside cars in driveways, on the ground in any outdoor location.

General Handling

Most outdoor bodies can be handled like a normal removal with some special considerations. If there is intense rain, follow the standards for waterlogged bodies.

Crowd Control ‚Äî Use the Police

Most outside deaths have police officers present. Police love to make crowds go away and they will listen to you.

You can tell them to do anything: "Make these people leave," "Shine your flashlights in people's faces," "Yell at them and clear this area." They will make it happen. Works every single time.

The Police Spotlight Technique (Nighttime): If there's an apartment complex with people watching from windows, tell police to flash their lights directly in people's windows. Makes people stop looking immediately. Nobody wants a spotlight in their face.

Environmental Awareness
  • Watch your surroundings: lumps and bumps in the ground, uneven terrain, obstacles
  • Look out for what you're rolling the cot over ‚Äî roots, rocks, holes
  • Try not to bring too much natural matter back with you (dirt, leaves, sticks, mud)
  • Be aware of everybody watching you ‚Äî family members, neighbors, people far away
  • Maintain professionalism even when being watched
Physical Demands

Outdoor removals can be exhausting ‚Äî hills to climb, long distances, hot or cold weather, rough terrain.

  • Stay hydrated ‚Äî bring water with you because it can happen at any time
  • You don't know when you'll get an outdoor call
  • Heat exhaustion is real
  • Take breaks if needed ‚Äî don't hurt yourself trying to rush

Finding the body: Outdoor locations can be hard to navigate. The coroner can send you GPS coordinates if you need a specific location. Ask for them and plug them into your phone.

Key Reminders ‚Äî All Special Cases

Bariatric Individuals: Poker face essential, never look scared, use mobile cot for weight distribution, partial human puppet technique acceptable, call fire department if extreme but you take control, no shame in family helping if capable.

Decomposition & Extreme Gore: Stock body bags and sheets, human puppet technique super helpful, control YOUR reactions (no retching, faces, crying), warn family professionally, try to prevent them from seeing extreme decomp, offer alternatives (covered viewing, prayer), you are the stability in the room.

Waterlogged Bodies: Skin slip is serious, human puppet technique or tuck and roll in tub, cut body bag bottom to drain water, good tread shoes essential, use bathroom fixtures for stability, don't let them slip and hit head.

Hanging Cases: Never just cut them down, support body before cutting rope, remove rope if going to funeral home, leave rope on if going to coroner (don't touch anything).

Outdoor Bodies: Rain means waterlogged protocols, use police for crowd control (they listen to you), police spotlight technique for nighttime watchers, watch environmental factors and terrain, stay hydrated (physically demanding), coroner can send coordinates.

Universal Principles:

  • You never know what you're walking into
  • Every situation is different
  • Stay professional no matter what
  • Control your reactions
  • Be the stability
  • Protect yourself and the deceased
  • When in doubt, call dispatch

Extreme Emotional Situations

The Golden Rule: Move Slow to Move Fast

You're trying to move fast ‚Äî get in, do your job, get out. BUT if you try to hurry too fast, you could cause an emotional breakdown. An emotional breakdown means being there way longer. Moving slowly and allowing time actually gets you out faster.

Why this matters: You can always leave and come back later if things are too intense. BUT what happens if an emotional breakdown occurs while the person is on your cot? You can't just leave all your equipment there and call it a day. Your equipment is there ‚Äî you're stuck.

The strategy: Move slow and with caution. Allow them extra time. Give them a second of silence. Whatever needs to be done so that you can move at all. Moving slower and progressing is better than a total stall from an emotional breakdown.

Situation #1: Intoxicated People

What you'll encounter: People get really drunk or really high as a coping mechanism. They can be hard to interact with ‚Äî slurred speech, emotional, unpredictable.

How to handle:

  • Treat them like you would treat your drunk college buddy, but without the dirty jokes
  • Accept that they are what they are
  • Be chill about it
  • Don't judge, don't lecture

‚ö†Ô∏è What NOT to do: Do NOT call the police or report drug use. That is not your place at all. You are there just to help them grieve.

If next of kin is too drunk to sign: Have somebody else sign the BTP. You should be okay.

Situation #2: Extreme Anger

What you'll encounter: Extreme anger directed at God, at you, at police officers ‚Äî sometimes anger is just directing itself at anything.

How to handle:

  • Do NOT take any anger, yelling, or behavior personally
  • Never yell back ‚Äî it does not help
  • Yelling back will only escalate the situation
  • Stay calm, stay neutral

Understanding anger: Anger can sometimes be redirected. It's often not really about you ‚Äî it's part of their grief process. Don't take it personally.

üö® If you feel fear or threatened: Leave immediately. Get out of the situation. If you are hurt or harmed, leave ‚Äî get out with your life. Do what keeps you safe. Call the police if the injury is bad enough or if you feel you need to do something about it.

Situation #3: Intense Grief (Non-Functional)

What you'll encounter: Person is so downtrodden with grief they cannot function. Not talking. Will not move. Sometimes barricaded themselves with the body in another room. Clinging to the loved one. Really intensely touching them. Laying on top of them.

The Best Option ‚Äî Get on Their Level

Example scenario: They are with their dad, laying on top of him on the floor.

  • Go sit on the floor next to them
  • Just shut up ‚Äî don't do anything

What might happen: Maybe they tell you to leave. If they do, leave. But they probably won't.

What to do:

  • Sit there
  • Hang out for a minute
  • Just talk to them
  • Be a person
  • Find out: What did this person mean to them?
  • Let them tell stories
  • Let them get out what they need to
  • Things will progress normally

Remember: You're moving slow, but you're moving slow to go fast. This is the golden rule in action. Give them the time they need. They'll move when they're ready.

Situation #4: Familial Conflict

Basic Arguments and Bickering

What you'll encounter: Family members start arguing. Bickering amongst each other. Tensions rise in the room.

How to handle:

  • For the most part, do NOT step in
  • Let it play out naturally
  • It will naturally create an opening for you
  • Use that opening to say what you need to say
  • Direct the conversation in the way you need it directed

Don't be a referee: Family drama is not your business. Let them work it out. Stay neutral.

Serious Fighting (Physical or Escalating)

What you'll encounter: Arguments get really serious. People actually start fighting. Domestic situations escalating.

How to handle:

  • Remove yourself from the situation
  • Wait
  • Most family fighting scenarios are short-lived and will resolve themselves
  • Some domestic situations can escalate seriously ‚Äî you will need to remove yourself for safety

Good news: Most of the time, you already have police there to help in those situations. You should be okay. Police can handle domestic disputes.

‚ö†Ô∏è Your priority: Your safety comes first. Don't get in the middle of physical altercations. Let police handle it if present.
